I have a long-running habit of starting side projects. The rush of a new idea, the excitement of setting up a fresh repo, the dopamine hit of that first commit — I love all of it. The hard part is sticking around for the boring middle.

Over the years I've started probably two dozen projects and finished maybe five or six. Here's what I keep learning (and re-learning).

## The scope trap

Every new project starts simple. "I'll build a thing that does one thing." Then you add another feature. And another. And suddenly your "simple chat app" has user authentication, file uploads, real-time notifications, and a bloated backend you're sick of maintaining.

The fix is brutal but necessary: **cut scope before you start coding.** Pick the absolute smallest version that still feels useful. Call it the "v0." If you feel embarrassed about how minimal it is, you're on the right track.

## Momentum beats intensity

I used to wait for a free weekend, then code for 12 hours straight. Burn out. Abandon the project. Repeat three months later.

What actually works for me is tiny, consistent sessions. 20 minutes. A single fix. One more feature. Push to GitHub. Done. The streak itself becomes the motivation — I don't want to break it.

## One core outcome at a time

Multitasking a side project is a death sentence. Working on the frontend, the backend, the deployment pipeline, and the README all at once means nothing ships.

I now pick exactly one thing per session. "Today I get the login page working." Not "today I work on auth." One concrete, finishable task. If I finish early, great — I stop. If I don't finish, I pick it up next time.

## Ship tiny iterations

The best thing I ever did was deploy early. Like, embarrassingly early. A bare HTML page with a title and a broken button. Then iterate.

Why? Because once something is live, it's real. It gives you a sense of progress that's way more motivating than a local file that nobody can see. And it forces you to deal with deployment issues when they're small, not when you have a 50-file project and no idea why it won't build.

## Some projects die. That's fine.

Not every idea deserves to be finished. I've learned to kill projects sooner rather than letting them sit in a "might come back to this" state for years. If I'm not excited about it after two weeks of consistent work, it probably doesn't need to exist.

The projects I did finish? They're the ones I kept coming back to even when the novelty wore off. The ones that solved a real problem I actually had — not a hypothetical one I thought was cool.

## Real examples from my own repo

Looking at my own GitHub pages site, the projects that shipped (FileSync, MovieApp, Insurance Cost Predictor, CSS Image Track) are all small, focused, and solve something I genuinely needed. The ones I abandoned were, without exception, scope-crept attempts at "the next big thing."

That's the lesson I have to keep re-teaching myself: build for yourself, ship early, and keep it small enough that you can finish it in a weekend.
