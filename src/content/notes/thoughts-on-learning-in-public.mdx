I used to think I needed to be an expert before I could write about something. That I had to fully understand a topic, have all the answers, and only then post about it. That mindset kept me quiet for years.

Eventually I realized: nobody learns from "here's the perfect solution I arrived at after years of experience." They learn from watching someone wrestle with a problem in real time.

So I started sharing small things. A scrappy script that solved a specific problem. A bug that took me hours to track down. A new tool I was trying out. Not polished tutorials — just notes from the trenches.

## What changed

- **I stopped second-guessing.** The fear of being wrong is way louder in your head than it is in reality. Most people are too busy with their own stuff to judge you for a typo or a wrong assumption. And the ones who do correct you? That's literally free feedback.

- **Explaining things forces clarity.** Writing something down has a way of exposing the gaps in your understanding. Half the time I think I know something, then I try to explain it and realize I don't. That's a sign I need to go deeper — not a sign I shouldn't write.

- **The public log keeps me honest.** If I told the internet I'd build something, I'm way more likely to actually build it. It's embarrassing to have that "still working on it" update three months in a row.

## How I actually do it

I keep it simple. A short post about something I figured out. A gist with a useful script. Even just a tweet-sized observation. The bar is: "would past me have found this useful?" If yes, it goes out.

I don't overthink the format. I don't write for SEO. I don't worry about hitting the right keywords. I just write what I know, as clearly as I can, and hit publish.

## The honest truth

Some posts get zero engagement. Some get a random reply from someone halfway across the world saying "this helped me." That one reply makes it all worth it. Plus, my future self comes back to these notes more often than I expected — so even if nobody reads them, I'm still benefiting.

Learning in public isn't about being an authority. It's about leaving a trail of breadcrumbs for yourself and whoever else might be walking the same path.
